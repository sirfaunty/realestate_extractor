# PART I — THE CHAMBERLAIN ENGAGEMENT

## 1. The situation in one paragraph

Kraus-Anderson (KA) is the managing member of **Chamberlain Apartments LLC**, a 283-unit apartment property in Richfield, MN, financed in part with tax increment financing (TIF). KA's JV partner is **Inland Development Partners (IDP)**. KA has filed a 2026 property tax appeal that, if successful, produces meaningful long-term value to the LLC. **IDP opposes the appeal** and circulated an Excel analysis (authored by IDP managing partner Tom Shaver, April 2026) concluding the appeal is roughly "break-even." Our work shows that conclusion is wrong — the analysis contains 15 identifiable errors, all biased in the same direction — and that the appeal is in fact strongly value-positive. The engagement produced a four-document rebuttal package plus a live financial model.

## 3. Key facts established (the analytical foundation)

These are the load-bearing facts. Every deliverable rests on them. All are sourced — see Addendum B for citations.

### Deal structure / legal
- **TIF Note:** originally $7,142,377 at 4.60% interest. Balance entering 2026 = **$7,269,118.57**.
- **TIF Note is a "limited revenue obligation"** (Note §3): paid only from TIF receipts, no recourse to the LLC, no personal guarantee. **Any balance unpaid at maturity (2/1/2046) is forgiven.** This is critical — it means a tax appeal cannot create a default; the worst case is simply that less TIF flows.
- **TIF District last increment year: 2045** (TIF Plan §2-7 — 25 years after first increment in 2020). District terminates after 2045.
- **Minimum Assessment Agreement (MAA):** recorded as Hennepin County Doc T05543322. Section 4 establishes a **value floor of $43,835,000** — the assessor legally cannot value the property below this while the MAA is in effect. Applies as of January 2, 2021 and each January 2 thereafter until TIF termination.
- **Original Net Tax Capacity (ONTC): $37,179** — reverse-engineered from Ehlers' published figures (Shaver's model incorrectly used $0).

### The TIF mechanics (how an appeal actually flows through)
Chamberlain Apartments LLC is **both** the property-tax payer **and** the TIF Note holder. When a tax appeal lowers assessed value (TMV):
- Property tax paid drops by ~$0.01598 per $1 of TMV reduction (class rate 1.25% × tax capacity rate 1.27866).
- TIF received also drops — but only by ~89.64% of the property tax reduction (the City retains a 10% admin holdback + 0.36% state auditor fee regardless).
- **Net to the LLC while the Note is still being paid:** ~10.36% of the property tax reduction (the admin + OSA piece the LLC was never getting back anyway).
- **After the Note is paid off (or after 2045 decertification):** 100% of the property tax reduction flows to the LLC, because there's no offsetting TIF to lose.
- **The bulk of the appeal benefit accrues in the post-Note-payoff window through 2045 and beyond.** This is exactly what Shaver's 10-year-truncated model misses.

### Ehlers reconciliation (our credibility anchor)
Our model reproduces **every** unit-level figure in Ehlers' April 2026 Chamberlain TIF Update **within $86**:
- Baseline annual TIF: $746,544 (we match exactly)
- Annual TIF at MAA Floor: $588,232 published / $588,176 ours
- Annual gross TIF reduction at MAA Floor: −$176,312 published / −$176,368 ours
- TMV decrease at full appeal: −$11,031,000 (exact match)
- NTC reduction: −137,888 (exact match)

This reconciliation is the whole game with Ehlers — it means our methodology *is* the City's methodology.

## 5. What's wrong with IDP's analysis — the 15 errors

Shaver's model concludes the appeal is "break-even" (10-yr Net Economic Impact: $17,704 nominal / $4,367 NPV). It gets there through 15 errors. **All 15 bias toward the same conclusion** — that the appeal isn't worth doing. Good-faith modeling errors scatter in both directions; these don't. That pattern is itself a finding.

**Tier 1 — each one independently flips the conclusion:**
1. **10-year horizon truncation** — omits 2036–2045, where most of the value accrues
2. **Attorney fee charged every year** instead of once at settlement (overstates cost ~$300–400K NPV)
3. **8% discount rate** — far above the 5–6% institutional norm for a stabilized TIF multifamily asset
4. **Base NTC set to $0** instead of $37,179 — overstates baseline TIF ~5.7%
5. **Developer-share double-count** — applies the 90% factor AND a separate (1−Admin) AND (1−OSA), effective 80.7%
6. **MAA Floor scenario never modeled** — the single largest-benefit case is simply absent

**Tier 2 — methodology/framing:**
7. Wrong starting TMV ($54.866M 2025 value vs. $52.151M county-set 2026)
8. TIF reduction framed as a 1:1 ownership cost (ignores that 10.36% was never the LLC's to lose)
9. Sensitivity analysis flexes growth rate — the wrong variable; the appeal targets value, not growth
10. Cherry-picked 2.5% TMV growth assumption
11. Misrepresents the legal constraints

**Tier 3 — outright formula bugs:**
12. `Increment_Comparison!C3` = `MIN(B2, Inputs!B8)` where B2 is the text header "Market Value"
13. Off-by-one: "No Appeal TIF" column references prior-year TMV
14. "Appeal TIF" column literally copies the prior year's cell — flat for 8 straight years
15. The Year-6 off-by-one is replicated across all 7 sensitivity scenarios

## 6. The IDP motivation question (sensitive — handle with care)

KA leadership asked the obvious question: *if appealing is so clearly beneficial, why is our partner fighting it?* We addressed this **analytically, not accusatorially** — laying out observed facts and the hypotheses they support, explicitly labeled as hypotheses. This matters because the leadership memo is potentially discoverable.

**The observed facts:**
- All 15 errors bias the same direction (good-faith errors don't behave this way).
- In 2018, IDP sold ~50% of its membership interest to outside investors **without KA's knowledge or consent**.
- We have **no visibility into the economic terms of that 2018 assignment** — it's entirely possible IDP assigned those investors a *disproportionate* share of TIF Note proceeds (e.g., 75% of TIF for 50% of equity).
- IDP has been **hyper-focused on TIF payments/distributions since inception** — disproportionate to its share of overall LLC economics.
- IDP chose an 8% discount rate — the financial signature of a liquidity-constrained investor who needs cash sooner.
- IDP has **stonewalled multiple KA requests** over the past several weeks for IDP's entity-level operating agreement.

**The hypotheses these support (all explicitly unconfirmed):**
- IDP may be liquidity-constrained, making a near-term ~$158K/yr TIF reduction acutely painful even if long-term NPV is positive.
- IDP may have specific obligations to its 2018 investors underwritten on the no-appeal cash-flow profile.
- IDP may have assigned disproportionate TIF rights to those investors — so its effective TIF exposure exceeds its JV equity percentage.
- TIF Note proceeds may be pledged as collateral for IDP debt.

**Why the operating agreement matters so much:** that single document would confirm or eliminate nearly every one of these hypotheses. IDP's refusal to produce it — while simultaneously asking the LLC to forgo a $5.5M strategy on the strength of a 15-error analysis — is the reason obtaining it has moved from "useful diligence" to "necessary diligence."

# PART II — THE OWNERSHIP PLATFORM VISION

## 10. The core idea

Chamberlain was, in effect, a **manual prototype** of something much bigger. To produce that rebuttal package we had to:

1. **Extract** information from a large corpus of source documents (TIF Plan, TIF Note, Minimum Assessment Agreement, Assignment of TIF Note, Ehlers updates, county assessment records, valuation proformas, the JV operating agreement, IDP's own model).
2. **Reconcile and database** that information — every parameter we used traces to a specific document, section, and (where possible) page.
3. **Abstract it through a specific lens** — in this case, a financial/legal lens for a tax-appeal dispute, then re-cut for two different audiences (Ehlers-technical vs. leadership-strategic).
4. **Produce role-specific deliverables** from the same underlying facts.

The vision is to **build that as a system** — not a one-off for one property in one dispute, but a portfolio-wide platform. Call it the **Ownership Platform**.

## 11. What the platform does — the four-stage pipeline

### Stage 1 — Extract
Ingest every source document associated with a property or entity: loan docs, JV/operating agreements, leases, management agreements, TIF/incentive docs, title, insurance, tax records, appraisals, environmental, service contracts, estoppels, etc. Extract structured information from each — dates, parties, dollar amounts, rates, covenants, options, deadlines, defined terms.

### Stage 2 — Database (with citation/traceability)
Every extracted fact is stored as a record that **links back to its source** — the document, the section/clause, ideally the page and a link to the document image itself. This is the part that makes the whole thing trustworthy: nothing is asserted without a traceable origin. The Chamberlain work already did this manually (Addendum B is essentially a hand-built version of this database for one property/one lens).

### Stage 3 — Abstract through lenses
The same underlying database gets re-cut for different **lenses** — each lens being a role/function with its own questions, vocabulary, and relevant subset of facts:

| Lens | Cares about | Example output |
|---|---|---|
| **Leadership / Reporting** | Portfolio-level value, risk, decisions needed | Board-ready summaries, decision memos |
| **Operational** | NOI drivers, capital needs, performance | Operating dashboards, variance reports |
| **Loan** | Debt terms, covenants, maturities, DSCR, triggers | Loan compliance calendars, refi timelines |
| **Partnership** | JV terms, consent rights, distributions, waterfall, capital accounts | Partner reporting, consent-requirement maps |
| **Legal** | Obligations, options, deadlines, defined terms, litigation exposure | Obligation registers, deadline tracking |
| **Leasing** | Lease terms, expirations, options, rent steps, recoveries | Rollover schedules, lease abstracts |
| **Management** | Service contracts, management agreements, vendor obligations | Contract registers, renewal calendars |

The point: **one database, many lenses.** A loan maturity date is a fact in the database; the Loan lens surfaces it as a refi deadline, the Leadership lens surfaces it as a portfolio risk, the Legal lens surfaces it as a covenant-compliance window.

### Stage 4 — Ownership Platform
The lenses roll up into a single platform that gives an owner **complete, end-to-end control over a portfolio** — every property, every entity, every document, every obligation, every deadline, every dollar — all traceable to source, all viewable through whatever lens the moment requires.

## 12. Why Chamberlain proves the concept

Chamberlain demonstrated every stage of the pipeline, by hand:

- **Extract:** We pulled the full document corpus (7+ batches) and extracted the load-bearing facts — the TIF Note terms, the MAA floor, the district timeline, the ONTC.
- **Database with citations:** Addendum B *is* the database for this slice — every parameter cited to a source document and section.
- **Lens abstraction:** The same facts produced a **technical/financial lens** (Memo #1, for Ehlers), a **leadership lens** (Memo #2), a **legal/forensic lens** (Addendum A), and a **partnership lens** (the IDP motivation analysis and operating-agreement diligence). Four lenses, one fact base.
- **Owner control:** The whole exercise existed because the owner (KA) needed to see clearly through the noise of a partner's misleading analysis. The platform generalizes exactly that need.

The Chamberlain dispute also surfaced *why* the platform matters: KA was at an information disadvantage to its own JV partner. IDP controlled the narrative with a model KA couldn't immediately rebut, and IDP is still withholding a document KA needs. An owner with the full picture — every document extracted, databased, and lens-ready — doesn't get caught flat-footed like that.

## 13. What would need to happen to build it

This is a sketch, not a project plan — the next conversation, not a settled roadmap.

- **Document ingestion + extraction engine.** The hardest technical piece. Real estate documents are heterogeneous, often scanned, full of defined terms and cross-references. Extraction has to be accurate and, critically, has to capture the *citation* (where each fact came from), not just the fact.
- **The data model.** What's the schema that holds "facts" in a way that's both queryable and traceable, and that supports every lens? This needs design before code.
- **Lens definitions.** Each lens is a specification: what questions does it answer, what facts does it need, what does its output look like? The seven lenses above are a starting list, not final.
- **The platform UI.** How an owner actually navigates portfolio → entity → property → document → fact, and switches lenses.
- **Trust + verification.** Because the whole value proposition is "traceable to source," there has to be a way to verify extractions and flag low-confidence ones. An owner betting decisions on this needs to trust it.
- **Pilot scope.** Chamberlain is the obvious first real property to run through a built system — we already have the corpus and the hand-built answer key to validate against.

## 14. Open questions for the platform (for partner discussion)

- **Build vs. buy vs. assemble?** Parts of this exist as point solutions (lease abstraction tools, loan covenant trackers). Is the platform a new build, an integration layer over existing tools, or a hybrid?
- **Who's the customer?** Is this an internal KA tool, a product for other owners, or both? That decision shapes everything about how it's built.
- **What's the wedge?** Seven lenses is the full vision. Which *one* lens, on which *one* portfolio slice, is the minimum viable thing that delivers real value and validates the approach? (The Chamberlain experience suggests the partnership/legal lens — JV terms and obligations — is acutely underserved and high-stakes.)
- **How does extraction accuracy get validated at scale?** The Chamberlain corpus was small enough to hand-verify. A portfolio isn't.
- **What's the relationship between the platform and the deliverables?** Chamberlain produced Word memos. Does the platform produce documents, dashboards, both, or something else?

